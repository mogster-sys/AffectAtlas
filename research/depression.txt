Depression — Perplexity Design Research
========================================
Source: Perplexity AI research, early 2026


VISUAL DESIGN SYSTEM AESTHETICS FOR DEPRESSION
================================================

Depression in visual design is usually conveyed through low-energy,
withdrawn, "drained" aesthetics: muted cool/neutral color, low
contrast, heavy or sinking compositions, and typography that feels
tired or constrained rather than dramatic.

Overall mood
Low arousal, low contrast: Depressed aesthetics feel flat, heavy,
and low-stimulation, avoiding bright, shouty, or highly saturated
visuals that can feel overwhelming to people in low mood.

Negative aesthetic bias: Depressed viewers more often judge faces as
"ugly" and are conservative in calling things beautiful, which
suggests designs that foreground discomfort, flatten beauty, or
deliberately withhold visual reward.

Color and light
Subdued, greyed hues: Desaturated blues, blue-grays, browns, and
especially greys are frequently used to symbolise depressed or low
mood. "Grey" was the dominant self-chosen mood color in anxious and
depressed groups.

Dull, darkened palettes: Dark or mid-dark, slightly muddy colors
feel more depressing or anhedonic than clear primaries; brighter
and cleaner hues are kept minimal or absent.

Avoid intense brightness: Young people with depression report that
overly bright, high-intensity colors feel shouty, painful, and
cognitively overloading, so calmer softer luminance is preferred.

Form, composition, and motion
Heavy, sinking layouts: Bottom-weighted layouts, elements that seem
to drop or sag, and dense stacking in corners communicate feeling
weighed down or falling into a void.

Static, repetitive forms: Simple, static shapes with blurred
overlapping edges or "foggy" layering can express exhaustion,
fatigue, and cognitive blur.

Low stimulation: Sparse compositions with few focal points and
minimal motion reduce sensory load and align with the withdrawn,
low-initiative state of depression.

Typography
Quiet, constrained type: Neutral sans-serifs or sober serifs in
regular or slightly condensed widths, moderate-small sizes, and
limited variation feel serious, low-energy, and subdued.

Distorted or off-kilter when symptomatic: In expressive work about
depression, off-kilter spacing, cramped or overly heavy type, and
blurred or partially obscured letters suggest mental fog, discomfort,
or self-directed hostility.

UX-specific considerations
Unobtrusive, blending style: Depressed young users often prefer
designs that visually "blend in" with other apps and do not
broadcast that they are using a mental health tool, to avoid stigma.

Therapeutic, not oppressive: Engagement with supportive aesthetics
can modestly reduce depressive symptoms, so depressed visuals in UX
are often balanced with small hints of calm or hope (e.g., a single
soft accent) rather than pure despair.


COLORS MOST ASSOCIATED WITH DEPRESSION
========================================

Design and psychology studies most consistently associate grey with
depression, with some work also implicating dull blacks and other
neutral, low-saturation tones.

Strongest association: grey
The Manchester Color Wheel studies found that anxious and depressed
participants overwhelmingly chose mid-dark grey swatches to represent
their current mood, while healthy participants tended to choose
yellow.

"Depressed people feel more gray than blue" — grey implies a dark,
monotonous, colorless life and disinterest in experience.

Neutral/dull colors and depression
Participants with the highest depression scores most often preferred
neutral colors (black, white, grey), with grey especially linked to
elevated Beck Depression Inventory scores.

Highly depressed individuals tended to avoid shiny or highly
saturated colors, preferring dull, low-chroma tones.

Role of "blue" and other hues
Blue was the most common "favorite" color even among depressed
participants, suggesting blue per se is not a reliable diagnostic
depression color, even though culturally "feeling blue" is linked
to sadness.

Dark, desaturated colors (including some blues) are more likely to
be read as sad or depressed than their bright saturated counterparts.

Design application
Products addressing depression often avoid bright yellows, oranges,
and reds, leaning on greys and muted tones to mirror users'
self-reported mood colors while adding softer calm accents.

Grey is strongly associated with feelings of depression, emptiness,
and formality, especially when used in large flat areas without
contrasting accents.


TYPOGRAPHY AND PERCEPTIONS OF SADNESS/DEPRESSION
==================================================

Typography can make text feel noticeably sadder by lowering visual
energy and fluency.

Typeface features linked to sadness
Weight and width: Wide/extended bold styles were significantly
associated with sadness and fear, while ultra-light weights were
linked to desire and more positive feelings.

Condensed or cramped forms increase reported dissatisfaction,
boredom, and negative affect, bleeding into a general sad or
"pressed-in" tone.

Legibility, fluency, and mood
Reduced legibility (odd letterforms, overly decorative styles, poor
spacing) lowers processing fluency; disfluent text tends to feel
less pleasant and more effortful, which readers may interpret as
serious, heavy, or emotionally negative.

Irregular or hard-to-parse forms dampen positive emotional responses.

Serif vs sans, curves vs angles
Serif faces are broadly perceived as serious, traditional, and
solemn, reinforcing sadness when combined with low-energy layout.

Angular, sharp forms feel more serious and harsh; rounded soft-edged
letters read as friendlier and comforting, softening a sad message.

Scale, spacing, and emphasis
Small sizes, tight leading, and narrow measure make text feel
constrained or suffocating, supporting a sad withdrawn mood.

Minimal bolding or emphasis keeps visual "loudness" low; heavy
all-caps or very bold settings are read as urgent or aggressive
rather than quietly sad.


IMAGERY AND COMPOSITION SIGNALING LOW MOOD
============================================

Low-mood UI imagery and composition are typically quiet, sparse, and
low-stimulation.

Imagery style and content
Avoid cliched distress images: Guidelines for mental-health visuals
discourage stereotypical "head in hands" or hyper-dramatic photos,
recommending more subtle, non-stigmatizing imagery such as abstract
shapes, soft gradients, or ordinary scenes with subdued lighting.

Everyday but subdued environments: Natural scenes (trees, sky, water)
or simple interiors with soft even light and minimal clutter support
mood in people with depression and anxiety.

Scale, space, and placement
Small focal point in large space: A single card, icon, or
illustration surrounded by generous whitespace suggests withdrawal
or low energy without explicit "sad" symbols.

Bottom-weighted or "sinking" layouts: Key elements slightly low on
the canvas with more space above than below subtly conveys heaviness
or being weighed down.

Detail level and motion
Low visual detail: Simplified illustrations, minimal texture, and
reduced on-screen elements lower cognitive load and align with
concentration and motivation difficulties common in depression.

Gentle or minimal animation: Slow fades and infrequent motion,
avoiding rapid transitions or attention-grabbing effects.

Iconography and symbolic cues
Neutral, metaphorical icons: Mood-tracking and self-care apps use
neutral faces, simple curves, or metaphors (weather, battery/energy
level, waves) rather than extreme expressions.

Hopeful but quiet elements: Subtle hints of support or hope (small
"help" icon, gentle upward path illustration) so imagery
acknowledges low mood without reinforcing despair.


DESIGNING CALMING INTERFACES FOR DEPRESSION
=============================================

Calming interfaces use low-stimulation visuals, highly predictable
flows, and strong user control so the product feels gentle, safe,
and manageable.

Visual tone and motion
Soft muted palettes: Soft blues, greens, lavenders, and neutrals.
Avoid red-dominant or neon schemes that increase arousal.

Limit motion and visual noise: Slow minimal animations, no flashing
or flickering, simple backgrounds.

Layout and cognitive load
Simple linear flows: Short step-by-step tasks with few options per
screen reduce cognitive load.

Predictable navigation: Consistent button placement, clear
affordances, immediate but gentle feedback.

Language and interaction style
Validating, non-pressuring copy: Empathetic, non-judgmental language
that acknowledges difficulty. Avoid toxic positivity or
guilt-inducing prompts.

Small wins: Short achievable exercises and micro-tasks increase
self-efficacy and reduce avoidance.

Control, personalization, and safety
User control over stimulation: Adjustable color theme, font size,
notification frequency, animation intensity.

Privacy and crisis support: Clear data-use explanations, discreet
branding, easy access to crisis resources.

Supportive features
Gentle self-regulation tools: Breathing exercises, brief mindfulness,
mood logging with calm visuals and optional reminders.

Design for bad days: "Low-effort modes" (short sessions, "skip for
now," minimal notifications) so the app remains usable when energy
is especially low.


COLOR PALETTES TO AVOID FOR MENTAL HEALTH APPS
================================================

Overstimulating and alarming palettes
Strong red as base: Saturated reds increase physiological arousal
and are linked to danger and anxiety. Advised against as primary or
background colors.

Bright warm floods: Large areas of intense yellow, orange, or
magenta feel energetic and tip into restlessness or anxiety. Better
kept as small accents only.

Neon and clashing schemes
Neon and ultra-saturated palettes: Increase arousal and attention,
becoming sensory overload in mental-health apps.

Clashing complements at full saturation: Bright red-green or
blue-orange create strong visual tension.

"Too happy" or clinical looks
Overly cheerful UIs for low mood: Young people with depression
report discomfort with intensely bright color schemes, preferring
darker or more muted palettes that blend in.

Harsh clinical palettes: Pure white with sharp medical blues/greens
and little warmth feels sterile and stigmatizing.

Safer direction
Muted mid-saturation palettes: Softened blues, greens, and neutrals
with moderate saturation.

Reserve strong colors for small roles: Red and intense hues only for
compact error states, alerts, or CTAs, balanced by large calm base.
