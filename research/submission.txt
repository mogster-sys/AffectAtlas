Submission -- Perplexity Design Research
=========================================
Source: Perplexity AI research, early 2026


VISUAL DESIGN SYSTEM AESTHETICS FOR SUBMISSION
================================================

Submission is usually conveyed by aesthetics that feel yielding,
quiet, and low-agency: soft forms, low contrast, and deferential
hierarchy rather than bold, assertive moves.

Overall visual posture
Low visual dominance: avoid full-bleed, high-impact hero elements;
use smaller, contained components that feel like they "hold back"
rather than push forward.

Strong hierarchy that places "others" above "self": content about
the other party or environment should be visually emphasized over
the "speaker."

Stable, low-energy composition: centered or gently bottom-weighted
layouts, limited diagonals, and minimal dynamic angles.


COLOR
------

Muted, desaturated palette: soft neutrals, gentle blues/greens, and
greys communicate calm, deference, and low emotional arousal.

Low contrast between foreground and background except where hierarchy
truly matters, to avoid aggressive "shouting" elements.

Warm accents only in very small doses (e.g., a soft beige or muted
terracotta) to keep the tone submissive rather than fearful or cold.


SHAPE AND FORM
---------------

Rounded, soft geometry: circles, rounded rectangles, and curved
borders suggest yielding, safety, and non-threatening intent.

Avoid sharp, spear-like triangles and extreme diagonals that imply
attack, speed, or dominance.

Use tapering or downward-softening shapes (wider top, narrower
bottom) to subtly echo bowing or lowering gestures.

Curves are read as non-threatening: circles and rounded forms are
associated with softness, comfort, and nurturing, so they feel
low-agency and non-aggressive.

People prefer curvature: experiments show that curved shapes are
rated as more pleasant and beautiful than angular ones, which
supports a more accepting, compliant vibe.

Rounded environments reduce tension: rounded art and interiors
correlate with more relaxed, cooperative, less aggressive behaviour,
which aligns with a submissive, deferent emotional tone.


TYPOGRAPHY
-----------

Lower contrast, humanist or rounded sans-serifs to feel approachable
and non-imposing.

Smaller sizes with generous line-height and letter-spacing; avoid
condensed, heavy weights that feel commanding.

Sentence case rather than ALL CAPS; minimal use of bold, and reserve
it for acknowledging others rather than self-assertion.


LAYOUT AND INTERACTION
-----------------------

Generous white space to signal restraint and respect, not emptiness.

Predictable, conventional patterns (e.g., simple vertical flows) that
communicate "I'm not trying to control you; I follow your
expectations."

Microinteractions that are slow and smooth (ease-in-out, longer
durations) so nothing feels abrupt or demanding.


DOMINANCE VS SUBMISSION CONTRAST
==================================

Dominance and power are the visual opposite of submission:

Color: dark bases with strong accents (black, deep navy, saturated
red, gold, white) -- high contrast, large fields of dominant color.

Shape: angular geometry (triangles, sharp corners, hard-edged
polygons), verticality and upward movement, solid blocks and dense
clustering.

Typography: bold, heavy weights, condensed or geometric grotesques,
tight deliberate hierarchy with emphatic titles.

Layout: clear focal dominance, asymmetrical balance with a "heavy"
side, strong grid and alignment suggesting control and discipline.

Motion: snappy, decisive transitions; scale and impact on entry;
limited but strong animation.

These contrasts help define submission by inversion: where power
pushes forward, submission pulls back. Where power is heavy and
angular, submission is light and curved.


ASYMMETRY AND BALANCE
======================

Asymmetrical balance gives controlled energy: unequal visual weights
arranged so composition still feels resolved and harmonious. This
creates movement and vitality without chaos.

Visual tension comes from pushing elements off-center, closer to
edges/corners, or closer together so spatial forces feel unresolved.
Tension raises arousal and drama; too much makes the design feel
unbalanced or anxious.

For submission: avoid strong asymmetric tension. Use centered or
gently balanced compositions that feel stable and non-confrontational.
Reserve asymmetry for dominant design contexts.

Asymmetrical designs tend to improve engagement because they create
controlled novelty and tension. But for submission contexts, the
goal is NOT engagement through tension -- it is comfort through
predictability and deference.
