Alarm -- Perplexity Design Research
=====================================
Source: Perplexity AI research, early 2026


VISUAL DESIGN SYSTEM AESTHETICS FOR ALARM
===========================================

Alarm is typically expressed through high-contrast, high-arousal
visuals: intense warm colors, sharp shapes, strong motion, and urgent,
high-priority hierarchy.

Core visual attributes
Color: Dominant reds and oranges signal urgency, danger, or immediate
action, often paired with dark or neutral backgrounds for contrast.

Contrast: Very high contrast between foreground and background to
demand attention and cut through visual noise (e.g., red on near-black
or white).

Saturation: Highly saturated primaries rather than muted tones to keep
arousal high and feel "emergency" rather than informational.


SHAPE AND LAYOUT
-----------------

Shape language: Angular forms (triangles, sharp chevrons, pointed
icons) feel more threatening and urgent than rounded shapes.

Motion: Rapid, repeating micro-animations (pulses, flashes, quick
scale or opacity shifts) cue urgency but should be short and sparing
to avoid overload.

Layout: Alerts occupy prominent, interruptive zones (top banners,
centered modals, sticky bars) with minimal surrounding content to
remove ambiguity.


TYPOGRAPHY AND COPY
--------------------

Typeface: Bold, high-weight sans-serifs with clean geometry support
legibility at a glance and reinforce a serious tone.

Hierarchy: Very clear headline (ALERT / WARNING) plus a single-line
explanation and one primary action, with reduced decoration and
minimal subtext.

Text treatment: All-caps or small caps for the label, tight line
lengths, and slightly reduced letter-spacing to keep blocks compact
and glanceable.


INTERACTION AND FEEDBACK
--------------------------

Sound: Short, sharp, high-frequency tones that trigger attention but
stop quickly; avoid continuous or repeating alarms to prevent anxiety
and habituation.

State changes: Clear before/after states (normal to alarm to resolved)
using distinct color shifts (e.g., blue/gray to red to green/neutral)
to mark transitions.

Acknowledgement: Strong visual feedback (button press ripple, banner
fade) when the user dismisses or responds to the alert, to reduce
lingering tension.


DESIGN SYSTEM ENCODING
------------------------

In a design system, "alarm" can be encoded as a specific tokenized
set of choices:

Color tokens: alarm/bg, alarm/fg, alarm/icon, alarm/border built on
red-orange hues with strict contrast ratios.

Component variants: Alert banners, toasts, modals, and badges with
severity="critical" or tone="alarm" variant that adjusts color,
border radius, and animation.

Motion tokens: An "urgent" motion curve (fast-in, short duration,
maybe 1-2 pulses) distinct from "gentle" or "informational"
animations.


DOMINANCE AESTHETICS (CONTRAST WITH SUBMISSION)
=================================================

Dominance and power read as visually assertive, high-contrast, and
control-oriented:

Color: Dark bases with strong accents (black, deep navy, saturated
red, gold, white) feel authoritative and high-status. High contrast
and large fields of dominant color reinforce visual control.

Shape: Angular geometry (triangles, sharp corners, hard-edged
polygons) signal strength, precision, and toughness. Verticality and
upward movement imply power and ambition. Solid blocks and dense
clustering look heavy, stable, and hard to move.

Typography: Bold, heavy weights communicate confidence and authority.
Condensed or geometric grotesques, or strong serifs feel serious,
institutional, and controlled. Tight, deliberate hierarchy with
emphatic titles.

Layout: Clear focal dominance with one primary hero element. Strong
grid and alignment suggest control and discipline.

Motion: Snappy, decisive transitions feel more powerful. Scale and
impact on entry. Limited, restrained animation -- a few strong
movements beat many fussy ones.


SEVERITY SCALE AND TOKENS
===========================

A concrete severity specification for alarm design systems:

Severity levels (low to high):
- info: status updates, confirmations (light blue, info-circle icon)
- success: completed actions (light green, check-circle icon)
- warning: potential issues (amber/yellow, warning-triangle icon)
- error: blocking app issues (red background tint, error-triangle)
- critical: high-impact, time-sensitive (strong red bg, white text,
  large warning icon)
- emergency: real-world threat to life (very dark red bg, white text,
  full-screen takeover)

Token patterns:
- Color: color.status.{severity}.{bg|border|text}
- Icon: icon.status.{severity}
- Border radius: decreases with severity (8px info down to 4px
  emergency -- sharper = more urgent)
- Border width: increases with severity (1px default, 2px critical)
- Motion: motion.status.appear.duration (150ms default), critical
  gets a pulse (400ms, scale 1.05), emergency gets fast overlay
  (120ms)

Typography:
- Status title: 600 weight, 14px/20px
- Status body: 400 weight, 13px/18px
- Emergency title: 700 weight, 18px/24px

Spacing:
- Inline padding: 12px horizontal, 8px vertical
- Icon gap: 8px
- Stack gap: 4px between title/body/action

Key principle: visual intensity should scale with severity. Reserve
the most aggressive visual treatment (full-screen red, pulsing icons,
ALL CAPS) for genuine emergencies to prevent desensitisation.
