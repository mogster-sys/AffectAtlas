Appalled — Perplexity Design Research
=======================================
Source: Perplexity AI research, early 2026


VISUAL DESIGN SYSTEM AESTHETICS FOR APPALLED
=============================================

Appalled generally maps to the disgust family of emotions in
affective design, so visual systems that feel contaminated, violated,
or "wrong" tend to express it best.

Core visual qualities
Disrupted order: Broken grids, misalignment, visual "errors," and
jarring proximity between elements can create a sense that something
is off or unacceptable.

Contamination cues: Visual metaphors of dirt, rot, stains, or
infestation (textures, overlays, image treatments) tap directly into
disgust/appalled responses.

Moral violation signals: Imagery or composition that implies broken
norms or threat (harsh contrasts, confrontational focal points)
strengthens an appalled reading.

Color system
Desaturated, "sickly" hues: Yellow-greens, murky browns, greyish
cyans tend to be associated with disgust and impurity rather than
calm nature.

Clashing combinations: Intentional disharmony (e.g., sickly green
against raw magenta or dirty orange) produces visual revulsion
rather than pleasant contrast.

Low-chroma backgrounds + sharp accents: Dirty neutrals with small,
aggressive accent colors (acid green, bile-like yellow) can make
specific elements feel contaminating.

Shape, layout, and motion
Irregular, spiky, or oozing forms: Jagged edges, drips, smears, and
warped contours visually echo bodily or environmental contamination.

Crowded or invasive layouts: Elements that press into each other,
overlap uncomfortably, or intrude into personal "space" create a
violated feeling.

Unsettling motion: Slight buzzing, jitter, delayed or sticky
transitions, and non-linear easing can make interfaces feel wrong
or unsafe, amplifying appalled responses.

Typography and imagery
Typographic dissonance: Distorted type, uneven baselines, cramped
leading, and harsh weight contrast undermine readability in a way
that feels abrasive rather than playful.

"Inappropriate" pairings: Combining refined serif or corporate sans
with grotesque or disturbing imagery heightens the sense of moral
or social violation.

Graphic detail of the "disgusting": Close-ups, texture emphasis
(mold, grime, bodily residues), or deliberately unflattering angles
reliably elicit disgust/appalled reactions.

System-level guidelines
Define appalled as: "high-arousal disgust with a strong violation
cue" so teams distinguish it from simple dislike or anger.

Encode it as a theme across tokens: Color tokens (polluted greens,
dirty browns, clashing highlights), shape tokens (irregular radii,
asymmetric spacing, broken grid), type tokens (distortion, cramped
tracking, weight shocks).


COLOR PALETTES THAT EVOKE SHOCK AND DISGUST
=============================================

Shock and disgust tend to emerge when "sick" or dirty hues are
combined with high contrast, harsh saturation, or unexpected pairings
that feel wrong or contaminated.

Key disgust colors
Yellow-green / green-yellow: Frequently linked specifically to
disgust (e.g., bile, rot, illness), especially at medium-low
lightness and medium saturation.

Dirty browns and murky olives: Brown is often associated with
disgust in color-emotion surveys, especially when dull and
low-chroma.

Greyed "sick" neutrals: Sludgy greys with a green or yellow cast
feel polluted rather than clean.

Colors that amplify shock
High-contrast complements: Opposites like electric blue vs maximum
yellow or red vs acidic green visually "shout" and can feel shocking
when pushed to full saturation.

Very dark backgrounds: Black and near-black carry strong negative,
high-power associations and intensify any toxic accent color.

Overbright accents: Maximum yellow or neon-like reds create sensory
overload, especially on dark or dirty bases.

Palette patterns
"Toxic hazard" palette: Near-black charcoal or very dark brown base
+ neon or high-saturation yellow-green and green-yellow accents.
Feels poisonous, biohazardous, contaminated.

"Rot and bile" palette: Muddy brown, khaki, and olive base + acidic
yellow-green, dull mustard accents. Strong association with decay,
dirt, and bodily fluids.

"Clinical wrongness" palette: Cold grey and off-white with greenish
tint base + small areas of sharp red and sickly cyan or
yellow-green. Feels like illness, infection, contaminated medical
environments.

How to tune shock vs disgust
To increase disgust, push hues toward brownish, yellow-green, and
dirty neutrals; lower chroma slightly so they feel more organic and
rotten than playful neon.

To increase shock, heighten contrast (light vs dark), use highly
saturated accents sparingly, and place them next to complements so
they visually clash.


TYPEFACE CHOICES FOR APPALLED EMOTION
=======================================

Appalled sits in the disgust-anger family, so type needs to feel
"wrong," abrasive, or contaminated rather than merely sad or scary.

Overall type personality
Display, not body: Appalled works best in expressive display faces
rather than neutral text families.

Low fluency, high tension: Fonts that are harder to process (awkward
shapes, uneven rhythm) generate cognitive strain and discomfort.

Structural features to use
Heavy or extreme weights: Very bold or ultra-black styles tend to be
linked with fear, dissatisfaction, and other intense negative states.

Condensed or distorted width: Condensed or irregularly scaled letters
create pressure and claustrophobia.

Sharp, angular details: Hard terminals, spikes, and broken curves
feel more hostile and unsettling than rounded humanist forms.

Rhythm, spacing, and texture
Uneven baselines and x-heights: Slight vertical misalignment or
inconsistent letter heights produce a subtle visual "gag reflex."

Tight tracking and cramped leading: Overly tight letterspacing and
line spacing heighten tension and perceived aggression, especially
in uppercase.

Rough, eroded, or smeared edges: Grunge or corroded textures on
strokes echo physical decay or contamination.

Combinations and contrasts
Inappropriate pairings: Combining formal serif or geometric sans
with disturbing imagery or "sick" color palettes increases the sense
of moral or social violation.

Abrupt style shifts: Sudden jumps between light and ultra-bold, or
between rigid caps and warped lowercase, make typography feel
unstable and confrontational.


IMAGERY STYLES THAT MAKE VIEWERS FEEL APPALLED
================================================

Imagery styles that make viewers feel appalled almost always tap
into disgust plus some shock: they suggest contamination, bodily
threat, or violated norms.

Disgust triggers in content
Pathogen/contamination cues: Rot, mold, spoiled food, bodily fluids,
infected wounds, parasites, filth, and unhygienic spaces are among
the most reliable visual elicitors.

Corruption of the clean or sacred: Dirty hospitals, contaminated
kitchens, desecrated religious or moral symbols, or ruined
bodies/clothing make viewers feel something pure has been violated.

Framing and proximity
Forced closeness: Tight macro crops on slime, sores, crawling
insects, or decay force the viewer into intimate contact, strongly
activating pathogen-disgust.

No safe distance: Filling the frame with the disgusting element
removes psychological distance and makes the threat feel immediate.

Texture, detail, and sensory suggestion
Hyper-detailed organic texture: Visible pores, cracks, pus, mold
spots, hair, and insects increase perceived "realness" and intensify
visceral revulsion.

Wet + wrong material mixes: Slime on metal, blood on porcelain,
waste on fabric — combinations that imply contamination of everyday
materials are especially appalling.

Human expression and body language
Disgust + shock faces: Wrinkled nose, raised upper lip, narrowed
eyes, head pulled back (disgust) combined with wide eyes or open
mouth (shock) communicate "appalled" instantly.

Recoil and avoidance: People leaning away, shielding themselves, or
pushing something out of view cue the viewer to share that reaction.

Stylistic treatments
Harsh lighting and sickly color: Strong directional light
emphasizing surface irregularities, paired with yellow-green, dirty
brown, or grey-green grading.

Minimal visual escape: Compositions with little neutral space where
disgusting content dominates most of the frame keep the viewer
locked in the appalled state.


SPACING AND COMPOSITION FOR ALARM
===================================

Spacing and composition increase alarm when they disrupt comfort:
crowding, imbalance, and edge-heavy layouts create tension that the
visual system reads as potential threat.

Crowding and low white space
Reduced white space makes information harder to parse and raises
cognitive load, increasing tension and alertness.

Visual crowding (many elements packed close together) impairs
recognition in peripheral vision and can feel overwhelming.

Asymmetry and imbalance
Deliberate imbalance (one side visually heavier) creates
compositional tension that feels unstable.

Breaking harmony rules (ignoring grids, golden ratio, regular
alignments) increases unease.

Proximity, edges, and "no safe zone"
Objects near or cut by the edge of the frame generate suspense,
implying motion off-screen or loss of control.

Elements pressed together or touching (tangencies) produce ambiguous
boundaries that feel unresolved and stressful.

Scale and contrast in composition
Extreme size contrast (one huge element crowding several small ones)
encodes dominance and vulnerability, reading as looming threat.

Dense focal zones against emptier areas pull attention sharply,
creating a "hot spot" of tension that can feel alarming.


TEXTURE AND CONTRAST FOR VISCERAL REACTIONS
=============================================

Texture and contrast can create visceral reactions when they push
surfaces and light-dark relationships toward the edge of comfort.

Texture that feels visceral
Rough and noisy overlays: Heavy grain, grit, and speckle increase
arousal and make UI surfaces feel abrasive or dirty, especially on
large panels.

Organic, imperfect materials: Scratches, smears, paper fibers, rust,
and concrete-like noise give digital elements a physical presence;
in darker or "sick" palettes, this reads as contaminated or unsafe.

Using contrast to jolt the viewer
High luminance contrast for urgency: Very bright elements on very
dark backgrounds produce strong visceral salience, pulling instant
attention.

Localized contrast spikes: Concentrating extreme contrast only around
critical elements creates hot spots that feel intense without
fatiguing the whole UI.

Combining texture and contrast in UI
Harsh contrast + coarse texture: Dark bases with bright saturated
accents overlaid with coarse grain or glitch textures — useful for
error screens, horror themes, or "hostile" modes.

Dirty mid-contrast zones: Mid-contrast backgrounds with uneven
mottled texture behind content suggest grime or wear.

Motion and state feedback
Textured error feedback: Brief bursts of grain, glitch, or noisy
overlays on error toasts turn feedback into a small shock.

Contrast pulses: Short subtle contrast pulses or flickers around
high-risk actions produce a gut-level "are you sure?" reaction.

Guardrails
Keep baseline surfaces smooth and medium-contrast for readability.
Reserve heavy texture and extreme contrast only for intentional
emotional peaks. Test with users for accessibility and fatigue —
over-bright, clashing, or highly textured interfaces can trigger
eye strain or migraines.
