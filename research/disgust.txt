Disgust — Perplexity Design Research
======================================
Source: Perplexity AI research, early 2026


VISUAL DESIGN SYSTEM AESTHETICS FOR DISGUST
============================================

Disgust in visual design is usually expressed through dirty,
contaminated, or distorted aesthetics: think rotten, slimy,
sickly, or bodily references, rather than just "ugly" or "bad."


CORE VISUAL LEVERS
-------------------
Semantic cues: Imagery of contamination (mold, rot, infestation),
bodily fluids, insects, decay, and spoiled food reliably elicit
disgust because they tap evolved avoidance mechanisms.

Context framing: The same disgusting motifs are more acceptable
and even enjoyable when framed as art, horror, or satire, where
users expect and "consent" to the experience.


COLOR AND TEXTURE
-----------------
Dark, dirty greens, olive tones, browns, and yellow-green are
repeatedly linked to rotten food, feces, and disgust in
color-emotion studies. Brown also associated with disgust more
than other basic colors.

Slimy, sticky, crusty, moldy, or granular textures trigger
contamination intuitions — rendered via noise, bump maps,
organic gradients, and gloss highlights.


FORM, COMPOSITION, AND MOTION
------------------------------
Asymmetric, bulbous, swollen, or deformed shapes read as
diseased or parasitic.

Clusters of small repeated elements (holes, bumps, spores)
can evoke trypophobia-like revulsion.

Overcrowded, cluttered layouts, visual "overgrowth," and
creeping encroachment toward the viewer reinforce invasive
contamination.

Slow oozing, pulsating, writhing, or dripping animations
amplify disgust more than fast, clean, ballistic motion —
they mimic organic decay or infestation.


UI-LEVEL DESIGN SYSTEM CUES
----------------------------
Color system: muted greys with sickly accent colors (dirty
green, bile-yellow, murky brown) and low saturation, reserving
rare saturated notes for shock moments.

"Contaminated" states: buttons or cards gaining stains, cracks,
or growing organic edges as the user makes "wrong" choices.

Uneasy affordances: hover states that feel slightly too sticky,
crowded, or encroached, rather than crisp and airy.

Iconography: Twists on standard icons (warning triangles that
look corroded, trash icons that appear overfull and leaking).


SAFELY USING DISGUST IN DESIGN
-------------------------------
Protective framing: Position disgust as playful, educational,
or aesthetic so users understand it as intentional and constrained.

Intensity control: Keep disgust localized and time-bounded
(specific screens, one-off effects) rather than pervasive —
negative cues strongly shape judgments and purchasing decisions.


COLORS THAT RELIABLY EVOKE DISGUST
====================================

Most reliable disgust hues
- Yellow-green / chartreuse: frequently rated as "sick" and
  "disgusting," linked to vomit, mucus, slime, illness.
- Olive and murky greens: darker, dirtier greens associated
  with rot, mold, stagnant water, contamination.
- Browns tied to decay: mid/dark browns linked to spoiled food
  and digested material.

Value and saturation patterns
- Low/medium saturation, dirty mixes: colors between green,
  yellow, and brown with muddied saturation are "pukey," "slimy."
- Moderate darkness: very dark tones lean toward fear/anger;
  middle-dark murky green-yellow/brown more reliably = disgust.

Practical guidelines
- Lean on yellow-green -> olive -> brown gradients with low
  saturation for disgust accents.
- Avoid clean, fresh greens and cheerful yellows; introduce
  brown/grey contamination, unevenness, or "swampy" shifts.


FORM AND TEXTURE PATTERNS SIGNALING ROT/DECAY
===============================================

Macro form cues
- Warping and deformation: sagging, bowing, swelling, collapsing
  silhouettes suggest internal integrity has failed.
- Fragmentation and loss: missing chunks, crumbling edges,
  flaking layers, exposed inner material.

Surface texture cues
- Cracks, fissures, peeling: networks of cracks, crazing,
  peeling paint, spalling surfaces.
- Soft, spongy, crumbly: porous, friable, powdery, or
  water-logged textures imply structural rot.

Biological overgrowth
- Fungi and mold: fuzzy growth, spots, patches, blooms,
  mycelium, mushrooms, shelf fungi, lichens.
- Stains and bloom patterns: irregular dark patches, halos,
  tide lines, mottled "bloom" textures.

Cluster and hole patterns
- Clustered bumps/holes: dense clusters of cavities, pits,
  nodules, blister-like bumps = infestation or disease.
- Trypophobic arrangements: repeated roughly circular elements
  with irregular spacing tap disgust in non-phobic viewers.

Applying in visual/UX design
- Introduce uneven erosion on edges, subtle crack maps, peeling
  overlays, and mold-like mottling.
- Use organic, non-uniform distributions: localized "hot spots"
  of damage rather than uniform noise.


LIGHTING AND CONTRAST FOR REVULSION
=====================================

Direction and placement
- Uplighting (from below): exaggerates eye sockets, nostrils,
  wrinkles — corpse-like, monstrous.
- Raking side light: brings out bumps, cracks, slime, pores —
  makes organic matter more tactile and repellant.

Contrast and exposure
- High contrast, minimal fill: deep dirty shadows, sharp edges
  around filth, wounds, or rot.
- Local hotspots on "gross" detail: overexpose wet/shiny/
  decomposing areas while surroundings stay darker.

Color and cast
- Sickly green or yellow casts: pushes skin toward jaundiced,
  infectious, poisoned looks.
- Dirty mixed color temperatures: cold blue/green ambience with
  warm sodium-like highlights = polluted, unsanitary spaces.

Temporal effects
- Creeping changes in cast/contrast: gradually increasing
  contrast or phasing in greenish cast makes environment feel
  like it is visually "rotting."


CULTURAL DIFFERENCES IN DISGUST-COLOR ASSOCIATIONS
====================================================

Shared tendencies
- Yellow-green / dirty green + some browns cluster near "disgusting"
  across many countries, reflecting pathogen-avoidance.

Where cultures diverge
- Brown-disgust links are stronger in German samples than
  non-European groups.
- White, black, red, yellow swing more with culture (mourning
  vs purity vs sacred).

Design implications
- For cross-cultural audiences, dirty yellow-greens and browns
  are relatively robust disgust signals.
- Pair with explicit cues of rot, stains, contamination rather
  than relying on color alone.
